# Downsizing in Savannah Without Downgrading

Most of the writing about downsizing makes it sound like a defeat. Smaller home. Less square footage. The kids are gone, the stairs are harder, time to scale back. The framing itself is wrong.

The right framing is *right-sizing.* You're not losing square footage. You're trading square footage you no longer use for something you actually want — proximity to the marsh, single-level living, a yard that doesn't punish you in August, walkability to a coffee shop you'd actually go to. Done well, downsizing in Savannah is one of the better financial and lifestyle decisions of your sixties.

I'm a Savannah-native Realtor and Certified Residential Appraiser of 21 years. Here's how to do it right.

## Start with what you're actually using

Before you call any agent, walk through your current home and answer one question per room: *when was the last time I actively used this room?*

Most clients I work with discover the same pattern. The formal dining room hasn't seen a meal in three years. The third upstairs bedroom is storage. The bonus room is half home office, half exercise equipment that gets used twice a year. The four-car garage has two cars and a lifetime of stuff.

If you're using 60% of your current home, you don't need to keep 100% of it. The right downsizing target isn't "smaller." It's "matched to actual use."

## What downsizing actually buys you in Savannah

The financial side is straightforward — sell larger, buy smaller, pocket the spread. The lifestyle side is where the real value sits.

**Single-level living.** Stairs aren't getting easier. A well-built single-level home — Pooler, Richmond Hill, Skidaway, Tybee 2nd-row — eliminates a problem before it becomes one.

**Lower maintenance.** Smaller yard, smaller footprint, fewer bathrooms, less HVAC. The hours you used to spend on a 4,500 sqft home become hours you spend somewhere else.

**Proximity to what you actually want.** If you've spent your Sunday afternoons at Tybee for 20 years, maybe you should live there. If your daughter and grandchildren are in Pooler, maybe Bryan County's closer than Skidaway. Right-sizing is a chance to align home with daily life.

**Liquidity.** A right-sized home with right-sized carrying costs frees capital for travel, family, healthcare planning, or simply the buffer you've earned.

## What an appraiser-trained eye reads in a downsizing transaction

When I work with right-sizers, here's the specific lens.

### Your current home

The pre-list comparable analysis on your existing 3,500-5,000 sqft home tells you what the market currently supports for it. Most right-sizers haven't had a real comparable conversation in years and are working from a number from 2018. The market has moved. Sometimes in your favor; sometimes not. Either way, the actual number drives every other decision.

### Your target home

Different rules apply. Single-level homes in good neighborhoods are competitive. Inventory is thinner than the larger-home market. The bracket you'll pay matters more than the bracket you'll receive — most right-sizers spend more per square foot on the smaller home than they receive per square foot on the larger one.

### The math both sides

Build a real spreadsheet, not a guess:

- Likely sale proceeds (current home, after costs)
- Likely purchase price (target home, after costs)
- Net spread to your pocket
- Monthly carrying-cost delta (taxes, insurance, HOA, utilities)
- Annual lifestyle savings (less yard work, less HVAC, less commute, etc.)
- Tax considerations (capital gains exclusion, property tax homestead transfer if applicable)

A real downsizing makes financial sense in three or four ways simultaneously, not just one.

## The neighborhoods that work for right-sizing

Not every Savannah neighborhood downsizes well. The ones that consistently work:

- **The Landings (Skidaway)** — multiple villages with single-level options, mature community, established support infrastructure. Best for buyers who like gates and don't mind dues.
- **Pooler** — newer construction, single-level new builds common, lower property taxes than Chatham. Best for proximity to airport and grandchildren in growing areas.
- **Richmond Hill (Bryan County)** — quiet, lower taxes, marsh-side living, less density. Best for buyers who want quieter without giving up coastal access.
- **Tybee Island (2nd-row or marsh-side)** — single-level cottages exist, beach lifestyle, tight community. Best for buyers who already love Tybee.
- **Habersham (Coastal Carolina)** — neo-traditional new urbanism, walkable, single-level options. Best for buyers willing to consider a 20-minute drive into Savannah for a different community feel.
- **Ardsley Park bungalows** — some original 1,800-2,200 sqft bungalows downsize cleanly. Walkable, urban-feel right-sizing.
- **Whitemarsh / Wilmington Island** — older community, some single-level options, marsh-front pockets.

Specific sub-section depends on your daily life. We talk that through in the discovery call.

## Five things right-sizers commonly regret

After 21 years, the patterns repeat. Here's what to avoid.

1. **Buying smaller before selling larger.** Bridging mortgages and double carrying costs eat the savings.
2. **Underestimating storage.** A 5,000 sqft home accumulates stuff. A 1,800 sqft home will not absorb all of it. Edit before you move.
3. **Choosing the wrong neighborhood for the right reason.** "I love Skidaway" is a reason. "All my friends are at The Landings" is a reason. "It's gated and feels safe" is a reason. The wrong reason is "I think we should." Match the choice to actual life.
4. **Skipping the single-level test.** If single-level matters now or in five years, prioritize it now. Retrofitting accessibility into a two-story is rarely cheap.
5. **Pricing the larger home by hope.** A pre-list comparable analysis on the home you're leaving is the single most useful piece of pre-decision data. Most right-sizers skip it.

## The Pearl perspective

Right-sizing is one of the few moves in real estate where you genuinely can have both — better lifestyle *and* better financials. *Luxury without the pretense* applies here perfectly. The luxury is the freedom you buy by trading square footage you don't use for time and capital you do.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much smaller should we go?**
A: Depends on actual use, not a percentage. Most right-sizers I've worked with land between 50-65% of their previous square footage. The right number is whatever fits your actual rooms.

**Q: Will we lose money downsizing?**
A: Almost never. The math usually works out positive — the smaller home tends to cost less than the larger home generates, and the carrying-cost delta is favorable. Specific numbers depend on your specific homes; we model it together.

**Q: Should we rent for a year first?**
A: Sometimes yes — if you're considering a meaningful neighborhood change, a one-year rental in your candidate neighborhood reveals truths a Saturday tour won't. Less useful if you already know the neighborhood.

**Q: What about capital gains tax?**
A: Federal primary-residence exclusion applies up to $500K for married filing jointly if you've lived in the home 2 of the past 5 years. Speak with your CPA — gains above the exclusion threshold are taxable.

**Q: When's the best time of year to list our larger home?**
A: Spring (March-May) is the strongest demand window in Savannah for larger homes. Fall (September-November) is the second-strongest. The correct answer depends on your specific home and timing flexibility.

**Q: Do you specialize in right-sizers?**
A: Right-sizers and downsizers are one of the audiences I work with most. The dual credential (Realtor + Certified Appraiser) is especially relevant on the seller side, where pricing the home you're leaving is the single most consequential decision.
