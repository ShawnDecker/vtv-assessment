For content that builds awareness and audience but doesn't ask for a meeting.

## CTA structure rules

### Single CTA only

Every piece of content has ONE CTA. Two CTAs in the same piece = neither performs. If you can't pick one, pick the one furthest down funnel.

### Specificity wins

- "Pick a 30-minute slot" outperforms "Get in touch"
- "Book your pre-list comparable analysis" outperforms "Reach out"
- "DM me PEARL" outperforms "DM me"

### Action verb starts

- ✅ "Book," "Pick," "Save," "DM," "Tour," "Schedule," "Reply"
- ❌ "If you'd like to," "Should you wish to," "It would be great if"

### Match the channel register

- IG/TikTok: 4-8 word CTAs max
- Email: 8-15 word CTAs
- Blog/long-form: full-sentence CTAs OK
- Ad creative: 2-4 word button text + 6-12 word supporting line

### Permission-based language

Use "no pressure," "no agenda," "happy to," "if it's useful." This is Nikki's voice — invitations, not pitches.

---

## CTA testing protocol

For any new CTA pattern, test on at least 3 pieces of content before declaring it dead. Track:
- Click-through rate
- Action conversion (DM received, slot booked, etc.)
- Quality of the leads it generates (vanity metrics like clicks vs actual booked consults)

A CTA that drives 100 DMs of "what's your phone number" is worse than one that drives 10 DMs of "I'd like to discuss selling my Skidaway home in 90 days."

---

## When to escalate to Nikki

If a CTA is producing:
- High click rate but low quality (lots of clicks, no real conversation)
- Mixed signal (some great leads, some confused/pushed)
- Off-brand response (DMs that feel transactional instead of relational)

→ Bring to Nikki for review. The CTA is teaching the audience what to expect; if the wrong audience is showing up, the CTA is mis-calibrated.
